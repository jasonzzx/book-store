# Foxes and Crows

## The Fox and the Crow

A crow, having stolen a fine piece of cheese, flew up to a branch to enjoy it. A fox passing below caught the scent, and stopped, and considered.

"Good day, madam Crow," he called up sweetly. "How glossy your feathers are today — how bright your eye! I have heard it said your voice is the loveliest of all the birds. If only I might hear a single song, I would call you Queen of the Forest."

The crow, greatly flattered, lifted her head, opened her beak, and gave a loud *caw* — and the cheese dropped straight into the fox's waiting mouth.

"Thank you," said the fox, strolling off. "That was all I wanted. And let me give you something in exchange: never trust a flatterer."

> Flatterers are not to be trusted; they feed on those who feed on praise.

## The Fox and the Grapes

One hot afternoon a fox came upon a vine trained high along a garden wall, hung with ripe, dark grapes. His mouth watered. He backed up, ran, and leapt — and missed. He leapt again, and again, until he was sore and panting, and still the grapes hung far out of reach.

At last he turned away, lifting his nose as he went. "They're sour anyway," he said. "Probably full of worms."

> It is easy to despise what you cannot have.

## The Fox and the Stork

A fox once invited a stork to dinner, and, for a joke, served soup in a wide, shallow dish. The fox lapped it up easily, but the stork could only wet the tip of her long bill, and went home hungry while the fox pretended concern for her poor appetite.

Soon after, the stork invited the fox in return. She served a delicious-smelling stew — in a tall jar with a long, narrow neck. Her bill reached it easily; the fox could only lick the rim.

"Do not apologize," said the stork, as the fox slunk away hungry. "One bad turn deserves another."

> Do not play tricks you are not willing to have played on you.
