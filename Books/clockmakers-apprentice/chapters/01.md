# The Shop on Vintner's Lane

The town of Bellhaven had forty-one public clocks, and no two of them agreed.

The clock above the bakery ran eleven minutes fast, which the baker swore was the only reason his bread never burned. The clock at the ferry landing ran slow in the mornings and fast in the evenings, as if it disliked commuters. The great clock in the church tower struck thirteen at least once a month, and nobody had been able to explain it in living memory.

Visitors found this maddening. The people of Bellhaven found it perfectly ordinary, the way people anywhere stop noticing the smell of their own harbor.

Odile arrived on the last day of autumn with one suitcase, one letter of apprenticeship, and a pocket watch that had belonged to her mother. The letter directed her to the shop of T. Marchbanks, Clockmaker, at the crooked end of Vintner's Lane — a narrow storefront squeezed between a shuttered hat shop and a wall of ivy, its window crowded with clock faces that all told different times.

She checked her mother's watch against them out of habit. It disagreed with every one.

Inside, the shop ticked. Not with the tidy, unified tick of a well-run clockmaker's, but with hundreds of separate heartbeats, layered and syncopated, so that the room seemed to rustle like a field full of insects. Behind the counter stood a tall old man with white hair and ink-stained fingers, writing in a ledger.

"Odile Ferrand," she said. "The new apprentice."

Marchbanks looked up. His eyes went — quickly, precisely, like a man reading an instrument — to the watch chain at her waist.

"You're late," he said. It was two o'clock in the afternoon, and her letter had said to come at two. She started to protest, but he raised one finger and pointed, one by one, at four different clocks on the wall behind him.

"Late," he said, "by that one. Early by that one. Punctual by those two. The first thing you will learn in this shop, Mademoiselle Ferrand, is that the question is never *what time is it*. The question is *whose*."
