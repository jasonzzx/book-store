# The Unwound Room

The trade, it turned out, had a ledger.

Not the one on the counter — that was for invoices. The real ledger lived in a locked drawer, and every public clock in Bellhaven had a page. The bakery clock's page read: *+11 minutes. Set by Herr Brandt's father, 1961, the year the ovens were rebuilt. He baked by it for forty years. His son does not know why the bread comes out right. Do not correct.*

The ferry clock: *Slow at morning, fast at evening, net zero. Installed by the ferrymen's widows after the crossing of 1907. The morning boat waits four extra minutes for stragglers and makes it up on the tide. Do not correct.*

Page after page. Forty-one clocks, and every error in them was a decision — an inheritance, a memorial, an apology, a bargain between neighbors so old that only the clock remembered its terms. Marchbanks was not the town's repairman. He was its archivist. He kept the mistakes.

"A clock that merely tells the time," he said, "is a instrument. A clock that tells *someone's* time is a story. My master kept these pages before me, and hers before her. We fix what breaks and preserve what matters, and the whole art is telling one from the other."

At the back of the shop was a door Odile had assumed led to a cellar. It led instead to a small room without windows, and the room was full of clocks, and the room was silent.

None of them ran. Their hands stood at scattered hours; their pendulums hung dead straight; dust lay on them like snow on a field of stopped soldiers.

"The unwound room," said Marchbanks quietly. "When a story ends — when the last person who kept a clock's time is gone, and no one is left to be early or late by it — we do not correct the clock. That would be a kind of lie. We stop it, at its own time, and it rests here."

He touched one, a small carriage clock stopped at 4:12, and did not explain it, and Odile did not ask.

"The church tower," she said instead. "Why does it strike thirteen?"

For the first time since she had known him, Marchbanks smiled with his whole face.

"Ah," he said. "That one is *my* page."
