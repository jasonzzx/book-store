# Keeping Time

Sixty years earlier, Marchbanks told her, there had been two apprentices in this shop: himself, and a girl named Sofia Reyes, who was better than he was at everything except patience.

They had courted the way clockmakers court — arguing about escapements, leaving each other notes folded into the backs of pocket watches. And because they were young and the nights were long, they had made a pact about the church tower, whose bell counted the hours over the whole town: whenever one of them managed, on their maintenance rounds, to make it strike thirteen, it meant *meet me tonight, on the roof of the shop, and we'll count the stars instead*.

"The mechanism for it is elegant," he said. "A thirteenth pin on the count wheel that only engages when a certain lever is set. Sofia designed it. It took her a winter. The verger has been complaining about that bell for six decades, and four clockmakers' guilds have inspected it and found nothing wrong, because there is nothing wrong with it. It is the most precisely engineered love letter in Europe."

"What happened to her?"

"She grew impatient with Bellhaven, as I said she would, and went off to Geneva to build marine chronometers, and became moderately famous, and we wrote to each other for fifty-one years." He wound the shop's chronometer with three exact turns. "She died in the spring. Her page in the ledger is written. Her clock is chosen — the little carriage clock, you saw it, stopped at twelve minutes past four, which was the hour her train left, once, when I did not ask her to stay."

The shop ticked its hundred scattered heartbeats. Odile looked at her hands.

"Then why does the tower still strike thirteen?"

"Because I have not stopped setting the lever," said Marchbanks. "Once a month, on my rounds. Old men climb stairs slowly, but we climb them." He closed the ledger and slid it across the counter to her, together with the small brass key. "It will be your page eventually. Your rounds. Your decision, one day, whether that bell is still a story or has become merely an error. That is the whole trade, apprentice. Any fool with a loupe can make a clock right. We keep the ones that are wrong on purpose."

Odile took the key. Outside, across the rooftops, the church tower began to strike the hour, and the whole of Bellhaven paused in its business to count — some of them to eleven, some to twelve, each by their own reckoning — and on this particular evening, the bell went one stroke further, and somewhere in the town a very old man was already reaching for his coat.

She checked her mother's watch. Seventeen minutes ahead, faithful as a kept promise.

"When do we start?" she said.
