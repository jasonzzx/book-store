# Seventeen Minutes

For the first month, Odile was not allowed to touch a single clock.

She swept the floor. She sorted screws into drawers by sizes so fine she needed a loupe to tell them apart. She learned to make the black tea Marchbanks drank at ten, at two, and at whatever hour the church tower struck thirteen. Mostly, she watched.

What she watched made no sense.

A clock would come in — the bakery's, say, eleven minutes fast — and Marchbanks would open it with the tenderness of a surgeon, clean it, oil it, true its escapement, and regulate it with a master's hand. And then, when the clock was running perfectly, keeping time to the second against the shop's chronometer, he would take out a small brass key, make a final adjustment — and set it eleven minutes fast again.

He did this every time. A clock from the schoolhouse went back four minutes slow. The ferry clock went back with its strange morning limp deliberately restored; Odile watched him bend a compensation spring out of true with a pair of pliers, on purpose, with the concentration of a man tuning a violin.

On the last day of the month she couldn't hold it in.

"You're not fixing them," she said. "You fix them and then you *break* them again. Exactly the same way. Why?"

Marchbanks set down his loupe. He looked almost pleased, the way he did when a gear train came together on the first try.

"Tell me about your watch," he said instead. "The one at your waist. It runs seventeen minutes ahead. It has run seventeen minutes ahead since you walked into this shop, which means it is not broken — a broken watch drifts. Yours *keeps* seventeen minutes, faithfully, like a kept promise. I could regulate it to the second in half an hour. Shall I?"

Odile's hand went to the watch before she knew she'd moved.

Her mother had set it ahead all her life — *so we're never late for anything, little bird* — and had been late for everything anyway, laughing, unrepentant, seventeen minutes behind her own watch until the day she wasn't there to be late at all.

"No," said Odile.

"No," agreed Marchbanks. "Now you are ready to learn the trade."
